Chapter 1 — Introduction

1.1 Formal Methods

I. Concept

  • Formal methods use math for precise, unambiguous specifications.
  • Replace informal specs → checkable math.
  • Apply at all software-lifecycle stages: design, development, post-release.
  • Same role as math in civil engineering: precision, understanding, reasoning.
  • Already used in computing — prior applications: probability theory (performance models), context-free grammars (compilers), relational calculus (databases).
  • Why docs matter: bad prose buries key facts; design flaws surface too late → expensive or impossible to fix.
  • Net effect: cost reduction — formality can reduce total cost, not just raise rigour.
  • A spec is a stepping-stone to code via refinement.

1.3 The Z Notation

I. Concept

  • Four layers:
    1. Type system: one type per object; auto-checkable.

    3. Schemas: group declarations + constraints.
    4. Natural language: ties math to real world — judicious variable naming plus vital commentary keeps a spec obvious to the reader.
  • Set-theory toolkit: standard operators, set comprehensions, Cartesian products, power sets.
  • Z is one aspect of a broader framework: the notation sits inside a language of specs, proofs, and natural-language explanation.
  • Supports refinement → first a simple-data-type model of the desired behaviour, then a second model that respects the design decisions made — closer to implementation.
  • Not for: non-functional properties like performance, size, reliability, concurrency, or timing — those need complementary methods.

1.4 The Importance of Proof

I. Concept

  • Proof adds quality; feasible with “light touch.”
  • Book's two-fold intention: (1) proof adds quality to development; (2) proof is a feasible part of industrial formal-methods use.
  • Spec stage: surfaces hidden assumptions; understanding requirements deepens while constructing the proof.
  • Design stage: shows why correct → helps when reqs change. Includes consistency proofs and refinement proofs.
  • Implementation stage: code matches spec.

  • Spec-stage proof pays off: proof at the spec stage makes a significant contribution to software quality.
  • Formality spectrum: rigorous argument → semi-formal → fully formal.
  • Skill = match formality to stakes — know when proof is worth doing.
  • An untested spec can still be wrong; proof is the quality gate.

1.5 Abstraction

I. Concept

  • Abstraction = deciding what to include vs. leave out.
  • Underground map case study: in 1908 London's tube map was drawn geographically — accurate to the streets but cluttered and hard to read. In 1933 Harry Beck, an engineering draughtsman, redesigned it as the Beck Diagram: straight lines at 90° and 45°, coloured per route, centre expanded, outer distances compressed. The Publicity Department initially rejected the design as too revolutionary. Beck was paid just five guineas.
  • What Beck kept: connectivity — which stations link to which. What Beck dropped: concrete details like geography and real distances.
  • Six properties of a good spec (attested by the Diagram): (acronym: ACCUMC)
    1. Abstract — logical layout only.
    2. Concise — entire system on one sheet.
    3. Complete — every station shown.
    4. Unambiguous — one reading only.
    5. Maintainable — survived 60+ years of changes.
    6. Comprehensible — universally grasped.
  • Cost-effective: five guineas bought the whole specification.
  • A route on the Diagram = a theorem provable from the spec: if the spec says A connects to B, you can plan a journey.
