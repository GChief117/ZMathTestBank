Chapter 2 — Propositional Logic

2.1 Propositional Logic

I. Concept

  • Proposition: statement that is true or false, never both.
  • Five connectives: ¬ (not), ∧ (and), ∨ (or), ⇒ (implies), ⇔ (iff).
  • Precedence: ¬ tightest, then ∧, ∨, ⇒, ⇔ loosest.
  • Simple propositions join into compound propositions via connectives.
  • Truth value: each proposition evaluates to t or f.
  • Compound truth determined entirely by constituent truth values.
  • Part of propositional calculus, the logical language of Z.
  • Rules of natural deduction let us introduce and eliminate connectives to build proofs.

2.2 Conjunction

I. Concept

  • Conjunction: p ∧ q true exactly when both p and q are true.
  • Truth table: only the row t, t gives t; all others give f.
  • ⋀-intro: from p and q separately, conclude p ∧ q.
  • ⋀-elim1: from p ∧ q, conclude p.
  • ⋀-elim2: from p ∧ q, conclude q.
  • Conjunction is commutative: p ∧ q ⇔ q ∧ p.
  • Derived rule: proven once, then reused as a new inference rule.
  • If hypotheses are empty, the conclusion is a theorem.

2.3 Disjunction

I. Concept

  • Disjunction: p ∨ q true when at least one of p, q is true.

2.4 Implication

I. Concept

  • Implication: p ⇒ q false only when p true and q false.
  • Antecedent p is stronger than consequent q.
  • Strength ordering: false is strongest; true is weakest.
  • ⇒-intro: assume p, prove q, conclude p ⇒ q (discharges p).
  • ⇒-elim (modus ponens): from p ⇒ q and p, conclude q.
  • Vacuous truth: p ⇒ q is true whenever p is false.
  • Currying: (p ∧ q ⇒ r) ⇒ (p ⇒ (q ⇒ r)) — conjunction of antecedents can be separated.

2.5 Equivalence

I. Concept

  • Equivalence: p ⇔ q means p and q have the same strength.
  • Also called bi-implication: p ⇔ q means both p ⇒ q and q ⇒ p.
  • ⇔-intro: from p ⇒ q and q ⇒ p, conclude p ⇔ q.
  • ⇔-elim1: from p ⇔ q, conclude p ⇒ q.
  • ⇔-elim2: from p ⇔ q, conclude q ⇒ p.
  • Subsume rule: if p ⇒ q, then p ∧ q ⇔ p.
  • Derived rules involving equivalence can be reused like any inference rule.

2.6 Negation

I. Concept

  • Negation: ¬p true iff p is false.
  • false (contradiction): a proposition that is false in every situation.
  • ¬-intro: assume p, derive false, conclude ¬p.
  • ¬-elim: from p and ¬p, conclude false.

  • Proof by contradiction: assume p, derive false, conclude ¬p (same as ¬-intro).

2.7 Proof Trees

I. Concept

  • Proof tree: rules fitted jigsaw-wise; leaves at top, root at bottom.
  • Each horizontal line applies one inference rule; the rule name appears to the right.
  • Building top-down: start from the root (goal), ask how it was introduced.
  • Building bottom-up: start from leaves (known facts), eliminate toward the goal.
  • Discharged assumptions are written [p](i); the superscript links to the discharging step.
  • A derived rule is a proof tree packaged as a new reusable rule.
  • Two proof methods: truth-table proof (check all rows) vs. natural-deduction proof (build a tree).

  • Step 4 — Right subtree (prove \(\neg g\)): symmetric: assume \([g]^{(2)}\), build \(f \vee g\) via [∨-intro2], contradict. \[\dfrac{\dfrac{[g]^{(2)}}{f \vee g}\;[\vee\text{-intro2}] \quad \neg(f \vee g)}{\textit{false}}\;[\neg\text{-elim}] \;\xrightarrow{[\neg\text{-intro}^{(2)}]}\; \neg g\]
  • Step 5 — Complete tree: \[\dfrac{\dfrac{\dfrac{[f]^{(1)}}{f \vee g}\;[\vee\text{-intro1}] \quad \neg(f \vee g)}{\textit{false}}\;[\neg\text{-elim}]}{\neg f}\;[\neg\text{-intro}^{(1)}] \quad \dfrac{\dfrac{[g]^{(2)}}{f \vee g}\;[\vee\text{-intro2}] \quad \neg(f \vee g)}{\textit{false}}\;[\neg\text{-elim}]}{\neg g}\;[\neg\text{-intro}^{(2)}]}{\neg f \wedge \neg g}\;[\wedge\text{-intro}]\]
  • Verify: \([f]^{(1)}\) and \([g]^{(2)}\) discharged. \(\neg(f \vee g)\) is a leaf (hypothesis). Root = \(\neg f \wedge \neg g\). De Morgan 1 proven.

A network is secure when there is no intrusion or malware. Prove: \(\neg(i \vee m) \vdash \neg i \wedge \neg m\).

  • Step 1 — Goal: \(\neg i \wedge \neg m\) → need both negations, combined via [∧-intro].
  • Step 2 — Prove \(\neg i\): assume \([i]^{(1)}\), build \(i \vee m\) via [∨-intro1], contradict with \(\neg(i \vee m)\). \[\dfrac{\dfrac{[i]^{(1)}}{i \vee m}\;[\vee\text{-intro1}] \quad \neg(i \vee m)}{\textit{false}}\;[\neg\text{-elim}] \;\xrightarrow{[\neg\text{-intro}^{(1)}]}\; \neg i\]
  • Step 3 — Prove \(\neg m\): symmetric — assume \([m]^{(2)}\), build via [∨-intro2], contradict. \[\dfrac{\dfrac{[m]^{(2)}}{i \vee m}\;[\vee\text{-intro2}] \quad \neg(i \vee m)}{\textit{false}}\;[\neg\text{-elim}] \;\xrightarrow{[\neg\text{-intro}^{(2)}]}\; \neg m\]
  • Step 4 — Combine: \[\dfrac{\neg i \quad \neg m}{\neg i \wedge \neg m}\;[\wedge\text{-intro}]\]
  • Verify: both threats individually contradicted. The key insight: assuming either disjunct lets you build \(i \vee m\), which contradicts the premiss.

A student fails when it is not the case that exam or coursework passed. Prove: \(\neg(e \vee c) \vdash \neg e \wedge \neg c\).

  • Step 1 — Goal: \(\neg e \wedge \neg c\). Conjunction at root → prove each negation.
  • Step 2 — Prove \(\neg e\): assume \([e]^{(1)}\) → \(e \vee c\) via [∨-intro1] → contradicts \(\neg(e \vee c)\) → \(\textit{false}\) → [¬-intro] → \(\neg e\).
  • Step 3 — Prove \(\neg c\): assume \([c]^{(2)}\) → \(e \vee c\) via [∨-intro2] → contradicts → \(\neg c\).
  • Step 4 — Complete tree: \[\dfrac{\dfrac{\dfrac{[e]^{(1)}}{e \vee c}\;[\vee\text{-intro1}] \quad \neg(e \vee c)}{\textit{false}}\;[\neg\text{-elim}]}{\neg e}\;[\neg\text{-intro}^{(1)}] \quad \dfrac{\dfrac{[c]^{(2)}}{e \vee c}\;[\vee\text{-intro2}] \quad \neg(e \vee c)}{\textit{false}}\;[\neg\text{-elim}]}{\neg c}\;[\neg\text{-intro}^{(2)}]}{\neg e \wedge \neg c}\;[\wedge\text{-intro}]\]
  • Verify: derived rule [de Morgan 1]: \(\dfrac{\neg(p \vee q)}{\neg p \wedge \neg q}\). Reusable in any future proof.

V.1 Practice: Proof Tree Structure

In a proof tree, which nodes are at the top?

Correct. Leaves at top, root (conclusion) at bottom.

  • Goal: understand tree orientation.
  • Strategy: leaves = starting points; root = final result.
  • Tree: each line connects premisses (above) to conclusion (below).
  • Verify: duplicated leaves can be collapsed.

V.2 Practice: Rule Identification

Consider the step: \(\dfrac{p \wedge q}{p}\). Which rule was applied?

Correct. Extracting the first conjunct from \(p \wedge q\).

  • Goal: identify the rule from the tree step.
  • Strategy: premiss = \(p \wedge q\), conclusion = \(p\).
  • Tree: elim1 gives the left conjunct; elim2 gives the right.
  • Verify: intro goes the other direction (builds \(p \wedge q\) from parts).

V.3 Practice: Building Conjunction Trees

To prove \(p \wedge q \vdash q \wedge p\), which rules are needed?

Correct. Extract both parts, recombine in swapped order.

  • Goal: swap conjuncts.
  • Strategy: elim2 gets \(q\), elim1 gets \(p\), intro builds \(q \wedge p\).
  • Tree: three rules, one level deep.
  • Verify: no assumptions needed; pure elimination + introduction.

V.4 Practice: Building Conjunction Trees

In the conjunction commutativity proof tree, how many leaves are there?

Correct. \(p \wedge q\) appears twice — once for each elimination.

  • Goal: count leaves.
  • Strategy: each elim rule uses \(p \wedge q\) as its premiss.
  • Tree: two copies of the same leaf.
  • Verify: deduplicated, only one unique premiss remains.

V.5 Practice: Building Conjunction Trees

Consider: \(\dfrac{q \quad p}{q \wedge p}\). Which rule?

Correct. Two premisses combined into a conjunction.

  • Goal: identify introduction rule.
  • Strategy: two things above the line, conjunction below.
  • Tree: [∧-intro] builds from parts.
  • Verify: elim decomposes; intro constructs.

V.6 Practice: Building Conjunction Trees

Consider: \(\dfrac{p \wedge q}{q}\). Which rule?

Correct. Extracts the second conjunct.

  • Goal: identify which elim rule.
  • Strategy: conclusion = \(q\) = second part of \(p \wedge q\).
  • Tree: elim2 gets the right-hand conjunct.
  • Verify: elim1 would yield \(p\) instead.

V.7 Practice: Building Disjunction Trees

To prove \(p \vee q \vdash q \vee p\), which structural technique is used?

Correct. Case-split on which disjunct holds, then re-introduce swapped.

  • Goal: swap disjuncts.
  • Strategy: assume \(p\) → build \(q \vee p\) via intro2; assume \(q\) → build via intro1.
  • Tree: [∨-elim] with two sub-proofs.
  • Verify: both assumptions discharged by the elim rule.

V.8 Practice: Building Disjunction Trees

In a disjunction commutativity proof, if you assume \([p]^{(1)}\), which rule gives \(q \vee p\)?

Correct. \(p\) goes into the second position of \(q \vee p\).

  • Goal: from assumed \(p\), build \(q \vee p\).
  • Strategy: \(p\) is the second disjunct → intro2.
  • Tree: \(\dfrac{[p]^{(1)}}{q \vee p}\;[\vee\text{-intro2}]\).
  • Verify: intro1 would place \(p\) first, giving \(p \vee q\) instead.

V.9 Practice: Building Disjunction Trees

How many assumptions are discharged in the \(p \vee q \vdash q \vee p\) proof?

Correct. One for each case in the case analysis.

  • Goal: count discharged assumptions.
  • Strategy: [∨-elim] discharges both case assumptions.
  • Tree: both share superscript \((1)\) — same discharge step.
  • Verify: no undischarged assumptions remain → valid derived rule.

V.10 Practice: Building Disjunction Trees

Consider: \(\dfrac{p}{p \vee q}\). Which rule?

Correct. Known \(p\) placed as first disjunct.

  • Goal: build a disjunction from a known fact.
  • Strategy: \(p\) is the left disjunct → intro1.
  • Tree: \(q\) can be anything — doesn’t need to be proven.
  • Verify: intro2 would need \(q\) known and place it in \(p \vee q\).

V.11 Practice: Building Implication Trees

Consider: \(\dfrac{p \Rightarrow q \quad p}{q}\). Which rule?

Correct. Implication + antecedent → consequent.

  • Goal: identify modus ponens.
  • Strategy: two premisses: the implication and its antecedent.
  • Tree: [⇒-elim] fires the implication.
  • Verify: [⇒-intro] builds implications; elim uses them.

V.12 Practice: Building Implication Trees

In the currying proof, how many [⇒-intro] steps are needed?

Correct. Three nested implications = three intro steps.

  • Goal: \((p \wedge q \Rightarrow r) \Rightarrow (p \Rightarrow (q \Rightarrow r))\).
  • Strategy: each [⇒-intro] discharges one assumption.
  • Tree: innermost discharges \([q]^{(3)}\), middle discharges \([p]^{(2)}\), outermost discharges \([p \wedge q \Rightarrow r]^{(1)}\).
  • Verify: three discharged assumptions, no hypotheses → theorem.

V.13 Practice: Building Implication Trees

In the currying proof, the core step combines \([p]^{(2)}\) and \([q]^{(3)}\) using:

Correct. Reconstruct the conjunction from assumed parts.

  • Goal: build \(p \wedge q\) to feed into modus ponens.
  • Strategy: \([p]^{(2)}\) and \([q]^{(3)}\) → [∧-intro] → \(p \wedge q\).
  • Tree: then \([p \wedge q \Rightarrow r]^{(1)}\) and \(p \wedge q\) → [⇒-elim] → \(r\).
  • Verify: the assumed pieces are reassembled to fire the original implication.

V.14 Practice: Building Implication Trees

A proof with no undischarged assumptions proves a:

Correct. Empty hypothesis set = theorem.

  • Goal: classify the result.
  • Strategy: undischarged assumptions become hypotheses of the derived rule.
  • Tree: all discharged → no hypotheses → theorem.
  • Verify: the currying proof is a theorem; subsume has one hypothesis (\(p \Rightarrow q\)).

V.15 Practice: Building Implication Trees

The subsume proof tree has one undischarged leaf: \(p \Rightarrow q\). This means the derived rule:

Correct. Undischarged leaves are the hypotheses of the rule.

  • Goal: understand hypotheses in derived rules.
  • Strategy: the derived rule [subsume] has premiss \(p \Rightarrow q\) above the line.
  • Tree: conclusion \(p \wedge q \Leftrightarrow p\) below the line.
  • Verify: not a theorem (has a hypothesis), but a valid derived rule.

V.16 Practice: Building Negation Trees

In the de Morgan proof of \(\neg(p \vee q) \vdash \neg p \wedge \neg q\), the left subtree proves \(\neg p\) by:

  • Strategy: assume \(p\), derive \(p \vee q\), contradict with \(\neg(p \vee q)\).

  • Verify: assumption \([p]^{(1)}\) discharged by [¬-intro].

V.17 Practice: Building Negation Trees

In the de Morgan proof, the two subtrees (\(\neg p\) and \(\neg q\)) are finally combined using:

Correct. The goal is a conjunction: \(\neg p \wedge \neg q\).

  • Goal: combine two proven negations.
  • Strategy: each subtree gives one negation; intro combines them.
  • Tree: [∧-intro] at the root.
  • Verify: the root rule matches the major connective of the goal.

V.18 Practice: Building Negation Trees

  • Goal: identify negation elimination.
  • Strategy: two premisses that contradict each other.

  • Verify: [¬-intro] consumes \(\textit{false}\) to conclude a negation.

V.19 Practice: Building Negation Trees

Consider: \(\dfrac{\textit{false}}{q}\). Which rule?

Correct. From \(\textit{false}\), conclude anything.

  • Goal: identify ex falso.
  • Strategy: \(\textit{false}\) above the line, arbitrary \(q\) below.

  • Verify: \(q\) can be any proposition — that’s the power (and danger) of contradiction.

V.20 Practice: Building Negation Trees

Consider: \(\dfrac{[p]^{(i)} \quad \cdots \quad \textit{false}}{\neg p}\;[\neg\text{-intro}^{(i)}]\). What kind of rule is \([\neg\text{-intro}]\)?

Correct. Assumes \(p\), derives \(\textit{false}\), discharges the assumption to conclude \(\neg p\).

  • Strategy: discharge rules cancel assumptions; intro/elim rules don’t necessarily.

If you remove duplicate leaves from a proof tree, the remaining unique leaves are the:

V.22 Practice: Discharging Assumptions

Which of these rules is NOT a discharge rule?

Correct. [∧-intro] requires both premisses proven — no assumptions to discharge.

  • Goal: identify discharge vs. non-discharge rules.
  • Strategy: discharge rules cancel bracketed assumptions.
  • Tree: [⇒-intro], [¬-intro], [∨-elim] all discharge.
  • Verify: [∧-intro] just combines two known facts.

A proof tree has root \(q \wedge p\) and leaves \(p \wedge q\). What derived rule does it represent?

  • Strategy: premiss = deduplicated leaves; conclusion = root.
  • Tree: \(p \wedge q\) → \(q \wedge p\) = commutativity.
  • Verify: disjunction commutativity would have \(\vee\) not \(\wedge\).

Once a derived rule is proven, it can be:

Correct. A derived rule is as valid as any basic rule.

  • Tree: [subsume], commutativity, de Morgan — all reusable.
  • Verify: derived rules simplify future proofs by encapsulating common patterns.

V.25 Practice: Truth Table vs Proof Tree

Which is an advantage of natural-deduction proof trees over truth tables?

Correct. Proof trees yield derived rules; truth tables just verify.

  • Goal: compare proof methods.
  • Strategy: truth tables check all rows — brute force; proof trees build reusable structure.
  • Tree: a truth table for 3 variables has 8 rows; a proof tree may be much shorter.
  • Verify: both methods are valid; trees are more expressive.
